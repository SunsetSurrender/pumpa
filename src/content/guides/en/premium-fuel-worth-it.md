At most pumps, the premium nozzle costs €0.15–0.20 more per litre than
regular. On a 45-litre tank that's **€7–9 extra, every fill**. Do you get it
back? For most drivers, no — because premium fuel doesn't do what most people
think it does.

## What octane actually is

The number on the pump — 95, 98, 100 RON — measures **resistance to knock**:
the fuel's ability to withstand compression without igniting on its own,
before the spark plug fires. That's it.

Higher octane does **not** mean more energy. Petrol at 98 RON contains
essentially the same energy per litre as 95. It doesn't burn "cleaner" or
"stronger"; it burns *more controllably* under high compression. If your
engine doesn't create the conditions where knock is a risk, the extra octane
does nothing — the fuel never gets to use its one talent.

## Which cars actually benefit

**Engines that require it.** Some high-compression and performance engines
specify 98+ on the fuel-flap sticker. For these, premium isn't optional — the
manufacturer means it. Running them on lower octane invites knock; the ECU
protects the engine by retarding ignition, costing power and economy, and
sustained knock can cause real damage. If your sticker says 98, this article
is short: buy 98.

**Engines that can exploit it.** Some turbocharged engines *specify* 95 but
carry ignition maps that adapt to better fuel, gaining a few percent in power
or economy on 98. The gains are real but modest — typically low single digits
— and only in demanding driving.

**Everything else — most cars on the road.** An engine designed for 95 and
driven normally gains nothing measurable from 98. Independent tests on
regular-spec cars keep landing on the same result: no meaningful difference in
consumption or power. The engine can't use the headroom.

## The break-even maths

Say regular is €1.80/L and premium €1.97/L — a 9.4% price gap. For premium to
pay for itself, it would have to *cut your consumption by more than 9%*.

A car doing 6.5 L/100km would need to drop below 5.9 L/100km — purely from the
fuel. Not even the adaptive-turbo case reliably delivers that; for a
regular-spec engine it's nowhere in sight. Even a generous 3% real improvement
on an adaptive engine leaves you paying ~6% more per kilometre for the
privilege.

Over 15,000 km a year at 6.5 L/100km, the premium habit costs about **€165
extra** — for, in most cars, nothing.

## The honest exceptions

- Your fuel flap or manual **requires** 98+: use it, always. That's not a
  premium habit, that's the spec.
- **Sustained heavy load in heat** — towing through mountains in summer with
  an adaptive engine — is where higher octane's knock margin can genuinely
  help. If that's your Tuesday, you're not who this article is warning.
- A **specific fuel's detergent package** (unrelated to octane) occasionally
  justifies itself in older engines — but that's an argument about additives
  in a brand's fuel, not about the octane number, and it's marginal.

## Don't argue — measure

This is the rare car debate you can settle yourself for the price of a few
tanks. Run three or four full tanks of regular, logging every fill-up with the
odometer reading. Switch to premium for three or four more. Pumpa's Fuel Log
computes your real consumption for both stretches from the odometer — not the
dashboard's estimate, not the forum's opinion: your car, your roads. If
premium doesn't move your number by more than the price gap, there's your
answer, printed on your own data.
