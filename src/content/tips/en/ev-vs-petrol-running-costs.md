Skip the purchase price, depreciation, tax breaks and the climate debate for a
moment. There's a smaller, sharper question underneath: **for the driving you
actually do, what does each kilometre cost in energy?** That one has a real
answer, and you can work it out on the back of a receipt.

## The petrol side, worked

Take an ordinary petrol car doing 6.5 L/100km, and fuel at €1.80/L:

> 6.5 L × €1.80 = **€11.70 per 100 km**

That's the whole calculation. Your own numbers will differ — a small city car
might do 5 L/100km (€9.00), an older SUV 9 L/100km (€16.20) — but the shape is
fixed: consumption × price. If you think in MPG, the same trip maths works in
US or UK units; the calculator converts properly either way.

## The EV side — where it gets interesting

An efficient EV uses around 17 kWh/100km in mixed driving. The catch: there is
no single electricity price. Where you plug in changes the answer completely.

**Charging at home** (typical domestic rate, €0.28/kWh):

> 17 kWh × €0.28 = **€4.76 per 100 km**

Less than half the petrol cost. On an off-peak or EV-specific night tariff
(€0.15/kWh in many markets), it drops to **€2.55** — roughly a fifth of petrol.

**Public AC charging** (€0.50/kWh):

> 17 kWh × €0.50 = **€8.50 per 100 km**

Still cheaper than petrol, but the margin has shrunk from "half price" to
"a decent discount".

**DC fast charging** (€0.70/kWh, common at motorway chargers):

> 17 kWh × €0.70 = **€11.90 per 100 km**

Read that again: at fast-charger prices, this EV costs **slightly more per
kilometre than the petrol car**. Nobody selling you either car says this
plainly, so we will: an EV that lives on motorway fast chargers has petrol-level
running costs.

## Your real number is a mix

Most EV owners charge mostly at home and occasionally fast-charge on longer
trips. Say 80% home, 20% DC:

> (0.8 × €4.76) + (0.2 × €11.90) = **€6.19 per 100 km**

That's the honest headline for a typical setup: **roughly half the petrol
cost — if you can charge at home.** No driveway or home charger? Run the mix
with your actual options before assuming anything.

## What it means over a year

A 15,000 km year, using the numbers above:

- Petrol at €11.70/100km: **€1,755**
- EV, mostly home charging (€6.19/100km): **€929**
- EV, fast-charging only (€11.90/100km): **€1,785**

The gap between the best and worst EV case is bigger than the gap between
petrol and the typical EV. The variable that matters isn't the badge on the
car — it's your access to cheap electricity.

## The honest "it depends"

- **Electricity prices swing more than fuel prices.** A tariff change can move
  your cost per km by 50% overnight, in either direction.
- **Winter matters more for EVs.** Cold weather can add 15–25% to consumption;
  petrol cars suffer too, but less.
- **This is running cost only.** Purchase price, depreciation, maintenance and
  insurance can each outweigh the energy gap. We're answering one question
  well, not five questions vaguely.
